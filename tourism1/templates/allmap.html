DIFFERENT TYPES OF INDIA MAPS



India's Political Map

States and Union Territories :

  • India has 28 states and 8 union territories.
  • The union territories of Jammu and Kashmir, Puducherry, and the National Capital Territory of Delhi have elected governments.
  • The other union territories are directly ruled by the central government.
  • Each state and union territory is divided into administrative districts, which are further divided into tehsils and villages.

Political Structure :

  • The states and union territories are based on a linguistic structure established in 1956.
  • Each state and union territory has its own culture, history, festivals, language, and dress.

Other Features :

  • India's coastline is 7,517 km long.
  • 5,423 km of the coastline is in peninsular India.
  • 2,094 km of the coastline is in the Andaman Nicobar and Lakshadweep islands.
  • India is bordered by the Indian Ocean, Arabian Sea, and Bay of Bengal.
  • India is divided into six main zones: Western, Southern, Eastern, Central, Northeastern, and Northern.




India's Physical Map

A physical map of India depicts the country's natural geographical features, including the Himalayan mountain range, the Indo-Gangetic plains, the Thar Desert, the Western and Eastern Ghats mountain ranges, major rivers like the Ganga, Yamuna, Indus, and Godavari, plateaus, and coastal areas, essentially showcasing the diverse topography of the Indian subcontinent; it distinguishes itself from a political map by focusing on landforms rather than state borders and cities.

Important Points to Remember about a Physical Map of India

  • Elevation: Different colors on the map usually represent different elevation levels, with:
    • Green for plains
    • Brown for mountains
    • Blue for water bodies
  • Scale: The map will be scaled to show the relative size and position of different geographical features.
  • Physiographic Divisions: A physical map often highlights different physiographic divisions within India, such as:
    • Northern Mountains
    • Northern Plains
    • Peninsular Plateau


Airways in India

Airways in India play a very important role because of their unmatched speed, long-distance operation, and time-saving lineaments. Air transportation is important as it helps reach the remotest areas, such as forests, deserts, mountains, etc., easily and in a lesser time span. It also helps in difficult situations such as famines, earthquakes, floods, epidemics, etc.

The first air mail service began in India in 1911. In 1920, some aerodromes were built in the country. In 1928, flying clubs were constructed in some cities.

Government Initiatives

Once the airlines began in India, the Government of India set up two different corporations in 1953:

  • Air India International – to cater to international air routes.
  • Indian Airlines – for domestic air transport.

Revolution in Air Travel

Air travel in India has undergone a revolutionary change ever since private airlines came into the picture.

Now, the proliferation of private airlines has considerably brought down fares that are now within the reach of common people.





India's Mineral Resources

Mineral Resources in India

India is one of the world's most naturally endowed lands. It is home to numerous minerals that benefit the country economically. The minerals produced in India constitute one-quarter of the world's most popular mineral resources.

India is gifted with important mineral resources. The country produces about 89 minerals, out of which:

  • 52 are non-metallic minerals
  • 4 are fuel minerals
  • 11 are metallic minerals
  • 22 are minor minerals

The total value of India's mineral production in the year 2000-2001 stood at Rs. 568070 million, excluding the value of petroleum and natural gas.

India's Mineral Production Rankings

  • Largest producer of mica blocks in the world.
  • 2nd largest producer of chromite, with a recent surge in demand.
  • 3rd in coal & lignite production.
  • 2nd in barites production.
  • 4th in iron ore production.
  • 5th in bauxite and crude steel production.
  • 7th in manganese ore production.
  • 8th in aluminum production.


Population Map of India

Definition: A population map of India represents the distribution, density, and demographic patterns of people across states and regions.

Total Population

Over 1.4 billion people, making India the most populous country (as of 2023).

High Population Density Areas

  • Uttar Pradesh – Most populous state (~230 million).
  • Maharashtra, Bihar, West Bengal, and Madhya Pradesh – Other highly populated states.
  • Delhi (NCR), Kolkata, Mumbai, Chennai, Bengaluru – Densely populated metro cities.

Low Population Density Areas

  • Arunachal Pradesh, Sikkim, Mizoram, Ladakh – Sparsely populated due to tough terrain.

Urban vs. Rural

About 35-40% urban and the rest in rural areas.

Tribal & Remote Populations

Found in Northeast India, Jharkhand, Odisha, and Chhattisgarh.

Growth Trends

Southern states (Kerala, Tamil Nadu) have lower birth rates, while northern states (Bihar, UP) show high population growth.





Land Use Map of India

Definition: A land use map of India represents how land is utilized for different purposes, including agriculture, forests, urbanization, and water bodies.

  • Agricultural Land

    Covers around 60% of India's total land, used for crops like wheat, rice, and pulses.

  • Forests

    About 21% of India's land is covered by forests, mainly in states like Madhya Pradesh, Arunachal Pradesh, and Chhattisgarh.

  • Urban Areas

    Cities and towns are expanding rapidly, with major urban centers like Delhi, Mumbai, and Bengaluru.

  • Industrial Zones

    Located in Gujarat, Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu, and West Bengal, supporting economic growth.

  • Barren Land

    Includes deserts, rocky areas, and wastelands, mainly in Rajasthan and Gujarat.

    • Water Bodies

    Lakes, rivers, and reservoirs, including the Ganga and Yamuna river basins.



Physiological Divisions Map of India

Definition: A physiological divisions map of India categorizes the country based on natural landforms and terrain characteristics.

The Northern Mountains

Includes the Himalayas, with high peaks, glaciers, and deep valleys. Acts as a climatic barrier.

The Northern Plains

Formed by the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra river systems, highly fertile and densely populated.

The Peninsular Plateau

Oldest landmass, includes the Deccan Plateau, Chotanagpur Plateau, and Malwa Plateau. Rich in minerals.

The Indian Desert

The Thar Desert in Rajasthan, with sandy terrain, low vegetation, and extreme climate conditions.

The Coastal Plains

Western and Eastern Coastal Plains, with fertile soil and major port cities.

The Islands

Andaman & Nicobar (volcanic origin) and Lakshadweep (coral atolls), significant for biodiversity.





Rainfall Map of India

Definition: A rainfall map of India shows the distribution of precipitation across different regions, influenced by monsoons, geography, and climate.

Monsoon Influence

The Southwest Monsoon (June–September) brings most of India’s rainfall, while the Northeast Monsoon (October–December) affects Tamil Nadu and parts of the east coast.

Heavy Rainfall Areas

The Western Ghats, Northeast India (Meghalaya, Assam, Arunachal Pradesh), and parts of the Himalayas receive over 200 cm of rainfall annually.

Moderate Rainfall Areas

The Indo-Gangetic Plains, central India, and eastern coastal plains get 100–200 cm annually.

Low Rainfall Areas

The Deccan Plateau and interior peninsular regions receive 50–100 cm.

Arid & Semi-Arid Regions

The Thar Desert (Rajasthan) and parts of Gujarat get less than 50 cm annually.

Cyclonic Influence

Coastal areas, especially in Odisha, West Bengal, and Andhra Pradesh, experience heavy rainfall from tropical cyclones.



Wind Map of India

Definition: A winds map of India represents the direction, speed, and seasonal variation of winds across the country.

Monsoon Winds

The Southwest Monsoon (June–September) brings moisture-laden winds from the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal, causing heavy rainfall.

Northeast Monsoon

Occurs from October to December, bringing winds from the northeast, affecting Tamil Nadu and the east coast.

Western Disturbances

Winds from the Mediterranean region, affecting North India during winter, causing rain and snowfall.

Local Winds

  • Loo – Hot, dry winds in North India during summer.
  • Kalbaisakhi – Thunderstorms in East India (West Bengal, Assam) in pre-monsoon season.
  • Mango Showers – Pre-monsoon winds in South India aiding mango ripening.

Cyclonic Winds

Coastal states like Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, and West Bengal frequently experience cyclones from the Bay of Bengal.

Trade Winds

Influence maritime weather patterns, impacting fishing and navigation.





Forests Map of India

Definition: A forests map of India shows the distribution and types of forests based on climate, topography, and vegetation.

Forest Cover

Around 21.71% of India’s land is covered by forests, classified into different types.

Tropical Rainforests

Found in Western Ghats, Andaman & Nicobar Islands, and Northeast India, with dense, evergreen trees.

Tropical Deciduous Forests

The most widespread, found in Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Odisha, and parts of Uttar Pradesh.

Thorn Forests

Located in Rajasthan, Gujarat, and Deccan Plateau, with drought-resistant vegetation.

Mangrove Forests

Found in Sundarbans (West Bengal), Odisha, and Andaman & Nicobar Islands, adapted to coastal conditions.

Himalayan Forests

Include coniferous and alpine forests in Jammu & Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, and Uttarakhand.

Protected Areas

Includes national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and biosphere reserves like Jim Corbett, Kaziranga, and Sundarbans.



Wildlife Map of India

Definition: A wildlife map of India shows the distribution of animal species, national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, and biosphere reserves across the country.

Biodiversity Hotspots

India has four major biodiversity hotspots – Himalayas, Indo-Burma, Western Ghats, and Sundaland (Nicobar Islands).

National Parks

Over 100 national parks, including:

  • Jim Corbett (Uttarakhand)
  • Kaziranga (Assam)
  • Sundarbans (West Bengal)
  • Ranthambore (Rajasthan)

Wildlife Sanctuaries

Over 550 sanctuaries, protecting species like tigers, elephants, and rhinos.

Tiger Reserves

53 reserves under Project Tiger, including Bandipur, Kanha, and Sundarbans.

Elephant Reserves

Found in Assam, Kerala, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu.

Biosphere Reserves

18 reserves, like Nilgiri, Nanda Devi, and Great Nicobar, preserving ecosystems.

Endangered Species

Includes Bengal tiger, Asiatic lion, one-horned rhinoceros, snow leopard, and Gangetic dolphin.

Bird Sanctuaries

Some notable bird sanctuaries include:

  • Bharatpur (Keoladeo)
  • Ranganathittu
  • Chilika Lake

These are home to various migratory birds.





Food Crops Map of India

Definition: A food crops map of India shows the distribution of major food crops grown across different regions based on climate and soil types.

Rice

The staple crop of India, grown in:

  • West Bengal
  • Uttar Pradesh
  • Punjab
  • Bihar
  • Odisha
  • Tamil Nadu

Requires high rainfall and fertile alluvial soil.

Wheat

Grown in:

  • Punjab
  • Haryana
  • Uttar Pradesh
  • Madhya Pradesh
  • Rajasthan
  • Parts of Bihar

Requires cool weather and well-drained loamy soil.

Maize

Cultivated in Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and Rajasthan. Grows well in moderate rainfall regions.

Millets (Jowar, Bajra, Ragi)

Drought-resistant crops grown in Maharashtra, Karnataka, Rajasthan, and Tamil Nadu.



Cash Crops Map of India

Definition: A cash crops map of India shows the distribution of crops grown for commercial purposes rather than direct consumption.

Cotton

Grown in Maharashtra, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Telengana

Requires black soil and a warm climate.

Sugarcane

Major producers include Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, and Bihar. Needs high rainfall and fertile soil.

Tea

Grown in Assam, West Bengal (Darjeeling), Kerala, and Tamil Nadu (Nilgiris). Requires cool, humid climate with well-drained soil.

Coffee

Mainly cultivated in Karnataka, Kerala, and Tamil Nadu in hilly regions with high rainfall.

Jute

Grown in West Bengal, Bihar, Assam, Odisha.

Requires humid climate and alluvial soil.

Tobacco

Grown in Andhra Pradesh, Gujarat, Karnataka, and Uttar Pradesh. Needs a dry climate.

Rubber

Cultivated in Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Tripura. Requires tropical, humid conditions.

Spices

Black pepper, cardamom, cloves, and cinnamon are mainly produced in Kerala, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu.





Industries Map of India

Definition: An industries map of India highlights major industrial regions, showing the distribution of key manufacturing and production hubs.

Major Industrial Regions

Mumbai-Pune Industrial Belt: Maharashtra; includes textiles, automobiles, chemicals, and electronics.

Delhi-NCR Industrial Region: Covers Delhi, Gurugram, Noida; specializes in IT, electronics, automobiles, and consumer goods.

Kolkata-Hooghly Industrial Region: West Bengal; known for jute, iron & steel, and heavy engineering.

Bengaluru-Chennai Industrial Corridor: Karnataka & Tamil Nadu; hub for IT, aerospace, and automobile industries.

Ahmedabad-Vadodara Industrial Belt: Gujarat; famous for petrochemicals, textiles, and pharmaceuticals.

Key Industries

Iron & Steel: Jamshedpur (Tata Steel), Bhilai, Rourkela, Bokaro.

Textiles: Surat, Mumbai, Coimbatore, Ludhiana.

Automobiles: Chennai, Pune, Gurugram, Sanand (Gujarat).

Petrochemicals & Refineries: Jamnagar (Reliance), Mathura, Vadodara.

IT & Software: Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Pune.

National Highways Map of India

Definition

A national highways map of India shows the network of major roads connecting cities, states, and important trade routes.

Total Length

Over 1.46 lakh km of highways managed by the National Highways Authority of India (NHAI).

Golden Quadrilateral (GQ)

Connects Delhi, Mumbai, Chennai, and Kolkata (5,846 km), boosting trade and transport.

North-South & East-West Corridor

  • North-South: Kanyakumari to Srinagar – 4,000 km.
  • East-West: Silchar to Porbandar – 3,300 km.

Major Highways

  • NH-44: Longest highway (Kashmir to Kanyakumari).
  • NH-16: Connects Kolkata to Chennai.
  • NH-48: Links Delhi to Mumbai.
  • NH-27: Longest east-west highway from Gujarat to Assam.

Expressways

  • Delhi-Mumbai Expressway
  • Yamuna Expressway
  • Mumbai-Pune Expressway
